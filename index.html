Retained Foetal Membranes (RFM)

An overview of one of the most common postpartum complications in dairy cattle, often called "retained cleansings," and its significant economic consequences.

What is RFM?

Defined as the failure to expel the foetal membranes (placenta) within 12-24 hours after calving. It's a sign of a dysfunctional transition period.

NZ Incidence Rate

Typically affects 5-15% of dairy cows in New Zealand herds, but this can be much higher in problem herds or during difficult seasons.

A "Gateway" Disease

An RFM dramatically increases the risk of other costly diseases, most notably metritis (uterine infection) and subsequent infertility.

The True Cost of a Single RFM Case

The economic impact of RFM extends far beyond the initial treatment. The hidden costs from lost production and reduced fertility are the most significant, with a single case costing over $500.

Understanding the Condition

RFM is not a disease itself, but a clinical sign that the complex process of placental separation has failed. This failure is often linked to impaired immune function around calving.

How Placental Separation Works (and Fails)

Normal Process: Pre-calving hormonal shifts trigger enzymes to break down the 120+ "buttons" (cotyledon-caruncle connections) holding the placenta to the uterus. Strong uterine contractions after calving then expel the detached membranes.

RFM Failure: This process fails due to poor uterine contractions (e.g., milk fever), or impaired immune function preventing the breakdown of the connections. The placenta remains firmly attached.

Cows vs. Heifers: A Tale of Two Risk Profiles

Key Risks for Mature Cows (Multiparous)

  • Higher Incidence: Mature cows generally have a higher incidence of RFM than heifers.
  • Metabolic Disease: The primary driver. Subclinical or clinical milk fever (hypocalcemia) is the biggest risk factor, as low calcium impairs muscle contraction, leading to weak uterine contractions.
  • Twins & Abortions: Events that stretch the uterus or cause abnormal hormonal changes significantly increase risk.
  • Nutritional Stress: Negative energy balance, poor body condition (too fat or too thin), and deficiencies in key micronutrients like Selenium, Copper, and Vitamin E all impair immune function.
  • Short Gestation Length: Cows that calve earlier than expected have a higher risk as the placental separation process may not be complete.

Key Risks for Heifers (Primiparous)

  • Lower Overall Incidence: Heifers typically have a lower baseline incidence of RFM compared to older cows.
  • Dystocia (Difficult Calving): This is the single biggest risk factor for heifers. A prolonged or assisted calving causes significant trauma, inflammation, and exhaustion, which directly interferes with placental separation.
  • Incorrect Bull Selection: Using bulls with high calving difficulty scores (EBVs) for heifers leads to oversized calves and a higher rate of dystocia.
  • Stress & Inexperience: The social and physical stress of entering the milking herd can contribute to a dysfunctional transition period.

Prevention: The Only Real Cure

RFM is a problem that starts weeks before calving. Prevention is entirely focused on ensuring a smooth and healthy transition period for the cow.

Core Pillars of RFM Prevention

1

Transition Cow Nutrition

This is the most critical factor. Work with a vet or nutritionist to formulate a proper "springer" diet that:

  • Manages calcium levels to prevent milk fever (e.g., using DCAD diets or low-calcium principles).
  • Avoids negative energy balance by providing adequate energy density.
  • Ensures sufficient levels of key micronutrients that support immune function, especially Selenium and Vitamin E.

2

Body Condition Score (BCS) Management

Cows should calve at the correct BCS (target 5.0 for mature cows, 5.5 for heifers). Over-fat cows are at high risk of metabolic problems, while under-conditioned cows lack the energy reserves for a healthy transition.

3

Minimize Stress

Stress hormones interfere with the normal calving process. Ensure springer mobs have ample space, easy access to feed and water, and are not subjected to unnecessary social stress from constant mixing of groups.

4

Calving Management

Provide a clean, dry calving area. For heifers, use easy-calving bulls to minimize dystocia. When assistance is needed, use good hygiene and proper technique to minimize uterine trauma.

Modern Treatment Approach

Veterinary thinking on RFM treatment has evolved significantly. The focus is now on managing the consequences and supporting the cow, not on forcefully removing the membranes.

Do NOT Manually Remove the Placenta!

Attempting to forcefully pull the membranes out is an outdated and harmful practice. It causes severe damage to the uterine lining, introduces infection, increases the risk of metritis, and delays the cow's return to fertility. The placenta will detach on its own when the underlying connections have broken down.
